Further explanation:

Consequently, the first dance, which is clearly the nucleus when identifying the four dances from the photographs, involves bamboos that are used to produce the beat as dancers move around the bamboos in time with the beat. Because molong is a sort of skirt that is typically worn in the stands and is what the individuals appear to be wearing, the second one resembles Gamelon Malone. The second 1 therefore consists of a couple of lengthy malone.

The third number is called Venus 1, since it is the number when ladies specifically balance cups on their heads and in their hands while dancing, as shown in the third figure. The fourth image suggests something moglaletic, so maglai. Since it is the dance in which men wear cocoanut shells essentially on top of their clothing or merely on their bodies somewhere and dance while the cocoanut shells are involved, as the fourth image shows, that is what the fourace are.

The simple vocal and instrumental music of Luzon's lowlands is essentially the result of the daily experiences of people like farmers, fisherman, artisans, vendors, and other regular people. Members of the family or community acquired this music orally and sung it in their own language. Most of the original composers of this music were unidentified.

Songs are written or sung in the local dialect and are based on the people's day-to-day experiences in a particular location. The lyrics of the songs must be easily understood in order to be appreciated. For instance, Tagalog folk music were created by Tagalog speakers. Leron Sinta, Bahay Kubo, and Magtanim ay "Di Biro" Ilocanos have their own dialect (Manang Biday and Pamulinawen). Among the folk songs of Luzon that are sung in their own dialects are the Atin Cu Pung Singsing of the Kapampangans and the Sarung Banggi of the Bicolanos.
