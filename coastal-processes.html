29. Ways to control coastal processes


Groins: Structures made of rocks, timber, concrete, old tires, etc. Project out from shore perpendicular to the beach. Sediment deposited on up-current side of groin and removed (eroded) on down-current side. Traps sediment from being moved farther along the beach by the longshore current. It solves the problem locally, but creates one (or makes it worse) someplace else.
Jetties: Similar to groins except much longer and placed to control inlet position and deposition. Beaches down current recede at very rapid rate: as much as 30 feet/year due to "sand starvation."
Breakwaters: Structures run parallel to beach. May also be submerged as an offshore reef. Create a protected area behind them. Used to create harbors or low energy swimming areas. Often filled in by deposition, causing greater erosion down current.
Seawalls (concrete), revetments (rock), and bulkheads (wood): Wave protection structures used to protect property behind beach. Beach often will disappear due to wave reflection at the base of the structure.
Beach replenishment: Pump sediment from offshore or up current where it is being deposited back on to the beach where it is being eroded. Usually a temporary and very expensive fix. In New Jersey, $3+ billion being spent to replenish coastline. In many cases 50% or more is lost in 1-3 years.
Dune reconstruction: Dunes are natural barriers for structures located behind them. They often are destroyed by development. Now trying to rebuild them. Start with sand fencing (causes deposition) and then plant beach grass once dune begins to form. Roots of beach grass hold sediment in place.
Relocation and Condemnation: Relocate buildings behind dune line. Do not allow reconstruction after damage
