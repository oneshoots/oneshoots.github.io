1. What is the electron configuration of potassium ​


Answer:

1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p6 4s1

Explanation:

Answer:

1s2,2s2,2p6,3s2,3p6,4s1.

In writing the electron configuration for Potassium the first two electrons will go in the 1s orbital. Since 1s can only hold two electrons the next 2 electrons for Potassium go in the 2s orbital. The next six electrons will go in the 2p orbital. The p orbital can hold up to six electrons. We'll put six in the 2p orbital and then put the next two electrons in the 3s. Since the 3s if now full we'll move to the 3p where we'll place the next six electrons. We now shift to the 4s orbital where we place the remaining electron. Therefore the Potassium electron configuration will be:

1s2,2s2,2p6,3s2, 3p6, 4s1.

Explanation:

The atom is made up of a nucleus containing the protons and the neutrons. Around it are the electrons moving around. The electron configuration and orbital diagram tell us the orientation or location of electrons.

Electron configurations tell us the location of electrons is around a nucleus. It follows a specific order, specifically, the electrons occupying the orbitals is based on the order of their energy. Specifically, it follows the order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p, 7s, 5f, 6d, 7p. It is important to note that to fill an s orbital, two electrons are needed; for the the p orbital 6 electrons are needed; for the d orbital, it needs 10 electrons; and lastly for f orbital, 14 electrons are needed. For instance, neon which has the atomic number, 10 has the electron configuration 1s²  2s²   2[tex]p^{6}[/tex] .

In addition, an orbital diagram is a visual representation of how electrons are arranged in an atom. Arrows in an orbital diagram represent the electrons of a specific atom or element. There are three rules that are utilized in orbital diagrams. These are the Auf bau principle, Pauli Exclusion Principle, and Hund's Rule. According to the Auf Bau Principle, the electrons occupy the lowest energy orbital first before occupying the highest energy. The Pauli Exclusion Principle states that only two electrons can fit into a single orbital. Hund's rule, on the other hand, states that orbitals of equal energy are each occupied by one electron before any orbital is occupied by a second electron and that each of the single electrons must have the same spin. Considering these rules, the orbital diagram is made.
