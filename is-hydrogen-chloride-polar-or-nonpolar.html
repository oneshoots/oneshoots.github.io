9. How do you classify carbon-containing compounds, polar or nonpolar? What makes it polar or nonpolar?


Answer:

Electrons are shared differently in ionic and covalent bonds.  Covalent bonds can be non-polar or polar and react to electrostatic charges.

Ionic bonds, like those in table salt (NaCl), are due to electrostatic attractive forces between their positive (Na+) and negative charged (Cl-) ions.  In unit two, we compared atoms to puppies and electrons to bones in our analogy of how bonding works. In ionic bonding, each puppy starts out with an electron bone, but one puppy acts like a thief and steals the other puppy’s bone (see Fig. 3-1a). Now one puppy has two electron bones and one puppy has none.  Because the electron bones in our analogy have a negative charge, the puppy thief becomes negatively charged due to the additional bone.

20. Table 2. Types of Covalent Bonds Chemical Formula Compound Lewis Structure Type of Bond (polar covalent/nonpolar covalent) ammonia water hydrogen chloride nitrogen gas oxygen gas methane hydrogen gas phosphine sulfur dioxide chlorine gas​


COVALENT BOND

    A covalent bond is formed when two atoms exchange one or more pairs of electrons. The two atomic nuclei are concurrently drawing these electrons to them. When the difference between the electronegativities of two atoms is too small for an electron transfer to take place to form ions, a covalent bond is formed.

Compound: Ammonia

Chemical formula [tex]NH_{3}[/tex] A single pair of electrons are perched on top of the nitrogen atom in the middle, which is hydrogen-bonded to three other atoms. Ammonia can transfer those electrons, which is why it functions as a Lewis base.covalent polar molecule - The electrons in the covalent bonds are drawn closer to nitrogen because it has a greater electronegative charge than hydrogen atoms. As a result, these bonds have an unbalanced distribution of electrons. Ammonia is a polar covalent molecule as a result.

Compound: Water

Chemical formula: [tex]H_{2}O[/tex]Lewis Structure: The oxygen and hydrogen atoms are connected by two single sigma bonds.Covalent Polar Molecule- A water molecule has two poles: a positive charge on the hydrogen pole (side) and a negative charge on the oxygen pole due to the unequal sharing of electrons between the atoms and the asymmetrical form of the molecule (side).

Compound: Hydrogen chloride

Chemical formula:  HCIThere are a total of 8 valence electrons in the hydrogen chloride (HCl) gas Lewis structure. Another name for HCl is hydrogen chloride.The hydrogen chloride covalent bond's bonding electrons are distributed unevenly.

Compound: Nitrogen gas

Chemical formula: [tex]N_{2}[/tex]When atoms share electrons evenly and the electrons do not spend more time around one atom than the other, a nonpolar covalent bond is formed. The molecule of oxygen gas (O2) has a nonpolar covalent link.

Compound: Oxygen gas

Chemical formula: [tex]O_{2}[/tex]Atoms share electrons equally and do not spend more time around one atom than the other, resulting in a nonpolar covalent bond. A nonpolar covalent link exists between molecules of oxygen gas (O2).

Compound: Hydrogen gas

Chemical formula: [tex]H_{2}[/tex]Hydrogen atoms share their electrons evenly, covalent molecules like hydrogen gas (H2), which only contains one type of atom, are nonpolar.

Compound: Phosphine

Chemical formula: [tex]PH_{3}[/tex]a non-polar bonding polar molecule. The electronegativity values of hydrogen and phosphorus are equivalent, as with three hydrogen bonds and a lone pair.

Compound: Sulfur dioxide

Chemical formula: [tex]SO_{2}[/tex]Sulfur and oxygen have a powerful double covalent bond. These two atoms are sharing two pairs of electrons.

Compound: Chlorine gas

Chemical formula: [tex]CI_{2}[/tex]Despite having a stronger electronegativity than hydrogen, the chlorine atom cannot remove an electron from hydrogen due to its inability to attract electrons. As a result, a polar covalent bond in hydrogen chloride has an unbalanced distribution of bonding electrons.
