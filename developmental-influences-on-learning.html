12. . Is the research findings on the influence of developmental and socio-cultural dimensions of learning important to the teachers and education practitioners? Why or why not?​


Answer:

The society and culture in which one grows up influence everything from developmental milestones and parenting styles to what kinds of hardship one is more likely to face. While biological milestones such as puberty tend to be universal across cultures, social milestones, such as the age at which children begin formal schooling or individuate from their parents, can differ greatly from one culture to the next. Effective parenting styles also vary as a function of culture. While the authoritative parenting style is the style that is most encouraged in modern American society, other cultures value more authoritarian styles. Race and racial stereotypes can have detrimental effects on a child’s development. Children are taught the stereotypes that go along with their race(s) and the races of others, and these stereotypes can have a strong influence on their development. Race is also closely linked to class, and children of color are still statistically much more likely to lack access to basic resources and to experience economic hardship. The concept of intersectionality is important to keep in mind when examining the cultural influences of various forms of discrimination on child development.

13. How do santrock developmental stages compare to havighurst's developmental stages? ​


Child Development

Santrock's list of developmental phases is identical to Havighurst's six developmental stages, with the exception that Havighurst omitted the prenatal stage. While Santrock refers to early childhood and infancy as two distinct stages, Havighurst integrated the two.

A kid passes through numerous phases of human development psychologically from birth to maturity. The ability to hold up one's head, the ability to communicate, and the ability to play are just a few of the developmental milestones that may help you determine whether or not your kid is developing at the appropriate rate.

One of child psychology's main interests is these milestones. Child psychology specialists frequently categorize developmental changes in a child's growth. They are able to explain why these changes occur and how a child's surroundings and culture effect these changes via the use of these divisions.

Santrock’s Developmental Stages

John Santrock's phases of development, which depict a person's complete life, are one of the more recent development theories in the area of psychology. According to Santrock's thesis, a person's life is divided into eight phases, including:

prenatal stage (infancy to birth)Infancy (birth to 18-24 months) (birth to 18-24 months)earliest years childhood (2-5 years)Middle and late childhood (6-11 years)Adolescenceyouthful adulthooda middle-aged adultlater in life

Havighurst’s Developmental Tasks Theory

According to Havighurst's view, people must complete one developmental stage before going on to the next. A person is encouraged to grow and feel more accomplished by peers and supportive communities. The phases of Havighurst's theory are as follows, along with the developmental activities they correspond to:

Early childhood (birth to five years): learning to walk, talk, and engage in social interactionsMiddle childhood (ages 6 to 12): Developing literacy skills and increasing independenceTeenagers (ages 13 to 17): Educated on gender-based responsibilitiesEarly Adulthood (18-35): Developing a profession and starting a familyAge between 36 and 60: Getting used to physiological changesLater Maturity (over 60): Getting used to retirement and old age.

14. How do you guide teachers in creating developmentally appropriate learning activities that build on prior knowledge?


Here are some steps for guiding teachers in creating developmentally appropriate learning activities that build on prior knowledge:

• Start with a clear understanding of the developmental stage of the students: Understanding the developmental stage of the students is critical in creating activities that are appropriate for their needs.

• Assess prior knowledge: Conduct pre-assessments or surveys to understand what the students already know and what they need to learn.

• Connect new learning to prior knowledge: Design activities that connect new learning to prior knowledge. This can be done through hands-on experiences, problem-solving activities, or other interactive experiences.

• Use graphic organizers: Encourage teachers to use graphic organizers to help students connect new information with what they already know.

• Foster a growth mindset: Emphasize that learning is a journey, and that building on prior knowledge is a critical part of that journey.

• Provide opportunities for reflection: Encourage teachers to provide opportunities for students to reflect on what they have learned and how they can apply it in new contexts.

• Evaluate and revise: Regularly evaluate the effectiveness of the learning activities and make changes as needed to ensure they are developmentally appropriate and building on prior knowledge.
