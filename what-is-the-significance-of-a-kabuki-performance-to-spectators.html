3. what is the significance of a kabuki performance to spectators?


Answer:

In 1629, Kabuki performance was tagged as a disruptive performance to the spectators by Japan government. They regarded this as unorthodox, an artistic performance with malice that is set to malign the minds of the people.

Explanation:

Kabuki is ka which means song, bu which means dance, and ki which means skill. It has been major traditional form of art in Japan. However, during the 17th century, a performer named Okuni made an unusual transformation on how the art is done. Okuni created parodies of Buddhist prayers. She assembled a group of female performers whose dance and acts are not the usual act.

Because of this, the government and most spectators wanted the act to stop. Kabuki was then suppressed in 1652 as it is disturbing the morals of the people.

Until time came when older men took the main role and continued the art. To date, the all-men performers is still being practiced with a more subtle atmosphere.

11. what is the message and functions of the kabuki performance?​


Answer:

Kabuki, traditional Japanese popular drama with singing and dancing performed in a highly stylized manner. A rich blend of music, dance, mime, and spectacular staging and costuming, it has been a major theatrical form in Japan for four centuries. The term kabuki originally suggested the unorthodox and shocking character of this art form. In modern Japanese, the word is written with three characters: ka, signifying “song”; bu, “dance”; and ki, “skill.”
