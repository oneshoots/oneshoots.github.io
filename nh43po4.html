9. What is the percentage composition of na3po4?


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of Na₃PO₄.

Note that the molar masses of Na, P, and O are 22.990 g, 30.974 g, and 15.999 g, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{molar mass of} \: \text{Na}_3\text{PO}_4 & = \text{3(22.990 g) + 30.974 g + 4(15.999) g} \\ & = \text{163.940 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Solve for the percentage composition of Na₃PO₄.

• For Na

[tex]\begin{aligned} \%\text{Na} & = \frac{n \times \text{molar mass of Na}}{\text{molar mass of} \: \text{Na}_3\text{PO}_4} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{3 \times \text{22.990 g}}{\text{163.940 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{42.07\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For P

[tex]\begin{aligned} \%\text{P} & = \frac{n \times \text{molar mass of P}}{\text{molar mass of} \: \text{Na}_3\text{PO}_4} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{1 \times \text{30.974 g}}{\text{163.940 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{18.89\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For O

[tex]\begin{aligned} \%\text{O} & = \frac{n \times \text{molar mass of O}}{\text{molar mass of} \: \text{Na}_3\text{PO}_4} \times 100\% \\ & = \frac{4 \times \text{15.999 g}}{\text{163.940 g}} \times 100\% \\ & = \boxed{39.04\%} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, Na₃PO₄ is composed of 42.07% Na, 18.89% P, and 39.04% O by mass.

---------------------------------------------------------

Note: The value of n in the formula of percent composition is the subscript of each element in the compound. If there is no subscript, it means that the value of n is 1.

11. What mass of phosphoric acid, H3PO4, would actually react with 7.17 grams of LiOH? 3LiOH + H3PO4 ® Li3PO4 + 3H2O


SOLUTION:

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of H₃PO₄ and LiOH.

The molar masses of H, P, O, and Li are 1.008 g, 30.974 g, 15.999 g, and 6.94 g, respectively.

• For H₃PO₄

[tex]\begin{aligned} MM_{\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4} & = \text{3(1.008 g) + 30.974 g + 4(15.999 g)} \\ & = \text{97.994 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

• For LiOH

[tex]\begin{aligned} MM_{\text{LiOH}} & = \text{6.94 g + 15.999 g + 1.008 g} \\ & = \text{23.95 g} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Step 2: Calculate the mass of H₃PO₄ reacted with LiOH.

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 3 moles of LiOH is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of H₃PO₄.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{H}_3\text{PO}_4 & = \text{7.17 g LiOH} \times \frac{\text{1 mol LiOH}}{\text{23.95 g LiOH}} \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{H}_3\text{PO}_4}{\text{3 mol LiOH}} \times \frac{\text{97.994 g} \: \text{H}_3\text{PO}_4}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{H}_3\text{PO}_4} \\ & = \boxed{\text{9.78 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Hence, 9.78 g of H₃PO₄ would actually react with 7.17 g of LiOH.

to balance an equation you need to know the following terms:

Coefficient which is a number that is written before the formula or the symbol of an element in an equation.

Subscript which is a number that is written slightly below a symbol of element.

in balancing equations you need to follow the following steps

1. write the unbalanced equation

2. list all elements present in the equation

3. identify and list the number of atoms of each element in both reactant side and the products side

4. write an appropriate coefficient that will balance the number of atoms of each element in the equation, this can be done looking at the subscript and giving a correct number that when multiplied by the subscript will balance the number of atoms of the element in both reactants and product.

Note: you cannot change the subscript because it will change the chemical compound in the equation.
